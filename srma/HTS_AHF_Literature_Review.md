## 1. Completed Randomized Controlled Trials

### 1.1 The Paterna/Palermo Group (University of Palermo, Italy)

This group, led by Salvatore Paterna and Gaspare Parrinello, has produced the bulk of the evidence. All trials enrolled refractory NYHA class IV patients with EF <35%, conducted at a single center or limited Italian sites. They used a consistent protocol: IV high-dose furosemide (typically 250–1000 mg/day, individualized) diluted in 150 mL of hypertonic saline (1.4–4.6% NaCl, concentration adjusted to serum sodium) infused twice daily, with a normosodic or moderate sodium diet (120 mmol/day). The comparator was typically the same furosemide dose given as IV bolus without HSS.

**Trial 1 — Paterna et al. (2000), Eur J Heart Fail**
PMID: 10938493 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/s1388-9842(00)00094-5)
- Design: Randomized, single-blind
- N = 60 (refractory NYHA IV CHF, EF <35%)
- Intervention: IV furosemide (500–1000 mg) + 150 mL HSS (1.4–4.6%) twice daily vs. IV furosemide bolus (500–1000 mg) twice daily
- Key endpoints: Diuresis, weight change, clinical improvement, mortality
- Duration of treatment: 6–12 days inpatient
- Note: This was among the first RCTs from this group. The abstract reports improved clinical signs and lab values in the HSS group. Long-term results were reported separately (see below).

**Trial 2 — Paterna et al. (2003), Am Heart J (Long-term follow-up)**
PMID: 12660669 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1067/mhj.2003.166)
- Design: Randomized, single-blind, long-term extension
- N = 107 (refractory NYHA IV CHF)
- Same intervention protocol as above, with a normosodic diet during follow-up
- Per the abstract: long-term follow-up showed benefits in mortality and readmissions in the HSS group. Specific numerical outcomes not fully detailed in the abstract.

**Trial 3 — Paterna et al. (2005), JACC**
PMID: 15963399 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jacc.2005.01.059)
- Design: Double-blind RCT
- N = 94 (refractory CHF, age 55–80, EF <35%)
- Intervention: IV furosemide (250–1000 mg) + 150 mL HSS twice daily vs. IV furosemide alone twice daily
- Primary endpoints: BNP plasma levels, bioelectrical impedance measurements (hydration status)
- Results: Per the abstract, the HSS group showed greater improvement in BNP and hydration parameters. This was their first double-blind trial.

**Trial 4 — Paterna et al. (2005), Clin Drug Investig (Torasemide comparison)**
PMID: 17523765 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.2165/00044011-200525030-00002)
- Design: Randomized, double-blind
- N = 84 (refractory NYHA IV CHF, EF <35%, age 55–84)
- Intervention: High-dose torasemide + 150 mL HSS vs. high-dose furosemide + 150 mL HSS (both arms received HSS)
- Key finding: Torasemide with HSS was equivalent to furosemide with HSS. Not an HSS vs. no-HSS comparison.

**Trial 5 — Paterna et al. (2011), J Card Fail (SMAC-HF Study)**
PMID: 21701268 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1097/MAJ.0b013e31820f10ad)
- Design: Single-blind RCT
- Population: NYHA class III (not IV as in prior studies), uncompensated, with EF <40%
- Intervention: IV furosemide (250 mg) + HSS (150 mL) over 30 min twice daily + moderate Na restriction vs. IV furosemide bolus + standard water restriction
- Endpoints: Hospitalization time, readmissions, mortality
- Abstract reports beneficial effects on short-term and long-term outcomes.

**Trial 6 — Paterna et al. (2011), J Card Fail (Echocardiographic outcomes)**
PMID: 21440872 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cardfail.2010.11.003)
- Design: Randomized trial
- Population: NYHA IV, EF <40%, unresponsive to high oral furosemide doses
- Intervention: HSS + high-dose furosemide + light Na restriction vs. high-dose furosemide alone
- Primary endpoint: Pulmonary capillary wedge pressure (PCWP) by Doppler echocardiography
- Results: HSS group showed improved PCWP estimates.

**Trial 7 — Paterna/Tuttolomondo et al. (2012), Am Heart J (Tra-HSS-Fur)**
PMID: 22980301 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ahj.2012.05.025)
- Design: Randomized trial
- N = 248 (ADHF patients, mean age 74.9 years)
- Intervention: IV high-dose furosemide + HSS (Group A) vs. IV high-dose furosemide alone (Group B)
- Primary endpoint: Troponin I release during treatment
- Key finding: Evaluated whether the HSS approach reduces myocardial injury markers. Per the abstract, troponin release was assessed as a marker of myocardial stress.

**Trial 8 — Tuttolomondo et al. (2015), Adv Ther (Dose-response)**
PMID: 26521190 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12325-015-0254-9)
- Design: Crossover RCT
- N = 42 (refractory CHF, consecutively hospitalized)
- Intervention: Patients randomized to furosemide 125/250/500 mg, first given with normal saline (0–24h), then the same dose with HSS (24–48h)
- Endpoints: Dose-response curves for diuresis
- Key finding: HSS improved the diuretic dose-response curve (shifted plateau upward).

**Trial 9 — Tuttolomondo et al. (2021), ESC Heart Fail (Biomarkers)**
PMID: 34288546 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1002/ehf2.13511)
- Design: Randomized
- N = 141 (ADHF with HFrEF)
- Intervention: IV high-dose furosemide + HSS (n=73) vs. IV high-dose furosemide alone (n=68)
- Endpoints: Heart failure biomarkers
- Per abstract: HSS group showed favorable changes in HF biomarker profiles compared to furosemide alone. Also evaluated saline loading in the compensated state.

**Trial 10 — Tuttolomondo et al. (2024), Clin Cardiol (3-arm bolus vs infusion vs HSS)**
PMID: 39558518 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1002/clc.70033)
- Design: Prospective randomized trial
- N = 1,276 patients admitted for ADHF
- 3 arms: (1) Continuous IV furosemide infusion, (2) Bolus furosemide injection, (3) Furosemide + HSS
- Duration: 48 hours of treatment assessed
- Note: This is the largest single RCT from this group. Abstract describes three therapeutic regimens compared. Specific outcome numbers truncated in the abstract retrieved.

### 1.2 Trials from Other Groups

**Issa et al. (2007), Arq Bras Cardiol (Brazil — Pilot)**
PMID: 17992382 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1590/s0066-782x2007001600007)
- N = 9 (pilot/case series)
- Population: Decompensated HF with congestion and hyponatremia
- Intervention: Standard treatment + hypertonic saline solution
- Primary aim: Renal failure prevention
- Very small pilot; safety/feasibility data only.

**Issa et al. (2012), Int J Cardiol (Brazil — RCT)**
PMID: 22243938 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijcard.2011.11.087)
- Design: Double-blind RCT
- N = unclear from abstract (likely ~40 based on registry NCT00555685 enrollment of 34)
- Intervention: 100 mL NaCl 7.5% twice daily for 3 days vs. placebo
- Primary endpoint: Increase in serum creatinine ≥0.3 mg/dL (renal dysfunction)
- Secondary endpoints: Creatinine, cystatin C, NGAL biomarkers
- **Notable:** This trial used a much higher NaCl concentration (7.5%) compared to the Paterna group (1.4–4.6%), though in smaller volume (100 mL vs. 150 mL). This is from the InCor/University of São Paulo group.

**Wan et al. (2014), J Card Fail (Japan)**
PMID: 24462960 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cardfail.2014.01.012)
- Design: Prospective, randomized, open-label
- N = 44 (consecutive ADHF patients)
- Intervention: 1.7% HSS + furosemide 40 mg vs. glucose infusion + furosemide 40 mg
- Endpoints: Diuretic effectiveness
- **Notable:** Used low-dose furosemide (40 mg) with 1.7% HSS — a different approach from the Paterna high-dose regimen.

**Li et al. (2017), Int Heart J (China)**
PMID: 28701670 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1536/ihj.16-313)
- Design: Open-label randomized
- Population: NYHA III, EF <40%, decompensated CHF
- Intervention: IV furosemide (100 mg) + compound HSS (100 mL) twice daily + water restriction (500 mL/day) vs. IV furosemide bolus (100 mg) twice daily + water restriction
- Endpoints: Hospitalization time, readmission, mortality
- Per abstract: evaluated hospitalization parameters and long-term outcomes.

**SALT-HF Trial — Cobo Marcos et al. (2024), Eur J Heart Fail (Spain)**
PMID: 39056478 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1002/ejhf.3390)
- Design: Multicenter, double-blind, randomized (13 Spanish sites)
- N = 167 (ambulatory patients with worsening HF, NOT hospitalized)
- Intervention: 1-hour IV infusion of furosemide (125–250 mg) + HSS (2.6–3.4% NaCl) vs. IV furosemide alone
- Primary endpoint: Volume of diuresis at 3 hours
- Secondary endpoints: 3h natriuresis, weight variation, 7-day congestion data, kidney function, electrolytes, 30-day clinical events
- Population: Median age 81 years, 50% HFpEF, high comorbidity burden
- **This is the most rigorous multicenter trial to date outside the Palermo group.** Published 2024.
- Post-hoc analyses also published: chloride repletion subgroup analysis (PMID: 39933832, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1002/ejhf.3597)) and natriuresis vs. diuresis prognostic comparison (PMID: 40152188, [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1002/ehf2.15275)).

**Griffin et al. (2020), JACC Heart Fail (U.S. retrospective)**
PMID: 32035891 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jchf.2019.10.012)
- Design: Retrospective cohort (NOT an RCT)
- Institution: Large U.S. academic medical center
- Population: Refractory ADHF patients receiving HSS for diuretic resistance
- Pre-post comparison of clinical trajectory before and after HSS administration
- **Notable:** One of the few U.S. real-world experience reports. Not randomized.

**Okuhara et al. (2023), J Card Fail (Oral NaCl — OSPREY-AHF)**
PMID: 37044281 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cardfail.2023.03.018)
- Design: Randomized, placebo-controlled, double-blind
- N = 70 (hospitalized AHF patients on IV furosemide)
- Intervention: Oral NaCl 2g three times daily vs. placebo during IV diuresis
- Primary endpoints: Change in serum creatinine and weight at 96 hours
- **Notable:** Tests oral sodium supplementation (not IV hypertonic saline) as adjunct. Related but distinct intervention.

### 1.3 The Barnes-Jewish Hospital Trial (Terminated)

**NCT00575484** — Washington University/Barnes-Jewish Hospital
- Phase 2, randomized, double-blind
- Intervention: Hypertonic saline + oral NaCl vs. normal saline + oral placebo
- **Status: TERMINATED** after enrolling only 11 patients (planned enrollment unclear)
- Terminated 2008 — likely recruitment difficulties.

### 1.4 The REaCH Trial (U.S.)

**NCT01028170** — Aspirus Heart and Vascular Institute, Wisconsin
- Phase 3, randomized, double-blind
- N = 50
- Intervention: High-dose furosemide + 2.4% HSS vs. pulse-dose furosemide
- Primary endpoint: Renal function (GFR) at 30 days
- Population specifically with pre-existing renal impairment (GFR <60)
- **Status: COMPLETED** (2011), but no published results identified in PubMed.

## 2. Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses

**Eight systematic reviews/meta-analyses were identified, all showing consistent favorable results for HSS + furosemide, though they draw predominantly from the Paterna group's trials.**

### 2.1 Wan et al. (2014), Int J Cardiol
PMID: 24679680 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijcard.2014.03.020)
- Included RCTs of HSS in acute CHF
- Pooled: 1,032 HSS-treated vs. 1,032 controls
- Findings: Decreased all-cause mortality, decreased HF readmissions, shorter hospital stay in HSS group

### 2.2 De Vecchis et al. (2014), Herz
PMID: 24682291 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00059-013-4041-6)
- Meta-analysis of HSS + furosemide vs. furosemide alone for ADHF
- Outcomes: All-cause mortality, re-hospitalization, LOS, weight loss, renal function, diuresis, serum sodium
- Found improved renal safety profile and clinical outcomes with HSS.

### 2.3 Defined/Medwave Review (2015)
PMID: 26335942 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.5867/medwave.2015.6233)
- Epistemonikos/GRADE summary
- Identified 2 prior systematic reviews including 9 RCTs
- Conclusion: HSS + furosemide probably decreases mortality, hospital LOS, and readmissions.

### 2.4 Eng et al. (2021), Crit Care Med
PMID: 34166286 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1097/CCM.0000000000005174)
- **Most comprehensive meta-analysis at the time**
- 11 RCTs, 2,987 ADHF patients
- Key results (per abstract):
  - All-cause mortality: RR 0.55 (95% CI 0.46–0.67; I² = 12%)
  - HF readmissions: RR 0.50 (95% CI 0.33–0.76; I² = 61%)
  - Hospital LOS: MD −3.28 days (95% CI −4.14 to −2.43; I² = 93%)
  - Daily diuresis: MD +583.87 mL (95% CI 504.92–662.81; I² = 76%)
  - Weight loss: MD −1.76 kg (95% CI −2.52 to −1.00; I² = 57%)
  - Serum sodium change: MD +6.89 mEq/L (95% CI 4.98–8.79; I² = 95%)
  - 24h urine sodium excretion: MD +61.10 mEq (95% CI 51.47–70.73; I² = 95%)
  - Serum creatinine: MD −0.46 mg/dL (truncated in abstract)

### 2.5 Dargham et al. (2021), Am J Cardiovasc Drugs
PMID: 33210263 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40256-020-00453-7)
- Systematic review and meta-analysis of HSS + furosemide in ADHF
- Evaluated mortality, readmissions, LOS, and physiological parameters

### 2.6 Yang et al. (2022), Comput Math Methods Med
PMID: 36188107 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1155/2022/5728967)
- Meta-analysis of HSS + furosemide for acute HF
- Acknowledged controversial efficacy; conducted new pooled analysis.

### 2.7 Hassan et al. (2023), Clin Cardiol (with Trial Sequential Analysis)
PMID: 37340592 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1002/clc.24033)
- 10 RCTs, 3,013 patients
- Used GRADE approach for quality of evidence assessment
- Included trial sequential analysis (TSA) for intermediate/biomarker outcomes
- Key results (per abstract):
  - Hospital LOS: MD −3.60 days (95% CI −4.56 to −2.64; QoE: moderate)
  - Weight loss: significant reduction (specific numbers in full text)
  - Mortality and readmissions also favorable
- **TSA was conducted — important for assessing whether the evidence has reached the required information size.**

### 2.8 Cochrane Review on Loop Diuretic Administration (2024)
PMID: 38775253 | [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD014811.pub2)
- Evaluated continuous infusion vs. bolus injection of loop diuretics in AHF
- Related but not specifically focused on HSS. Provides context on diuretic strategy comparisons.

## 3. Ongoing / Planned / Recently Completed Trials on ClinicalTrials.gov

### 3.1 SALT-HF (NCT04533997) — **COMPLETED, results published 2024**
- Sponsor: Hospital Puerta de Hierro, Spain
- Phase 3, multicenter (14 Spanish sites), double-blind
- N = 167 ambulatory patients with worsening HF
- Intervention: IV furosemide (125–250 mg) + HSS (2.6–3.4%) vs. IV furosemide alone
- Primary endpoint: 3-hour diuresis volume
- Published in Eur J Heart Fail (PMID: 39056478)

### 3.2 DAPA-TONIC (NCT06442280) — **NOT YET RECRUITING**
- Sponsor: University of Palermo (Paterna/Tuttolomondo group)
- Phase 4, 4-arm factorial design
- Planned N = 544
- Arms: (1) Furosemide + HSS, (2) Furosemide alone, (3) Furosemide + HSS + dapagliflozin, (4) Furosemide + dapagliflozin
- HSS: 100 mL, concentration adjusted to serum Na (1.4–4.6%), infused over 15 min twice daily
- Population: ADHF with type 2 diabetes, age 40–89
- Primary endpoints: Daily diuresis, natriuresis, hospital LOS
- Expected completion: Q1 2026
- **Notable:** Tests the interaction between HSS and SGLT2 inhibitors — a timely question given SGLT2i are now standard of care.

### 3.3 DECONGEST (NCT05411991) — **COMPLETED, has posted results**
- Sponsor: Vrije Universiteit Brussel
- Phase 4, multicenter (Belgium), open-label
- N = 107
- Not purely an HSS trial — tests a comprehensive urine sodium-guided diuretic algorithm that includes HSS as one component of a stepped approach
- HSS used as part of the diuretic resistance escalation protocol
- Primary endpoint: Hierarchical composite (mortality, days alive out of hospital, NTproBNP reduction at 30 days)
- **Results have been posted on ClinicalTrials.gov**

### 3.4 Monastir HSS Trial (NCT05298098) — **STATUS UNKNOWN**
- Sponsor: University of Monastir, Tunisia
- N = 600 (planned)
- Intervention: 50 mL of 10% NaCl + furosemide 250 mg vs. 50 mL dextrose 5% + furosemide 250 mg, twice daily
- Population: ADHF with renal dysfunction (CrCl ≤60 or Cr >150 µg/mL)
- Primary endpoints: Hospital LOS, in-hospital mortality, renal function impairment, need for inotropes
- Status listed as "Unknown" — may have been completed by now but no results published
- **Notable:** Uses 10% NaCl (very hypertonic) in 50 mL — the highest concentration in any registered trial.

### 3.5 NCT04628325 (Palermo — Atrial Stretching/Fibrosis) — **COMPLETED**
- Sponsor: University of Palermo
- Phase 3, N = 136 (HFrEF)
- Endpoints: Atrial stretching, fibrosis, and inflammatory markers
- Intervention: Furosemide + HSS vs. furosemide alone
- Completed 2019; publication likely corresponds to PMID 34288546.

### 3.6 NCT01419132 (Palermo — Troponin) — **COMPLETED**
- Sponsor: University of Palermo
- Phase 4, N = 60, double-blind
- Endpoints: Troponin I release
- Completed 2011; publication likely corresponds to PMID 22980301 (Tra-HSS-Fur).

## 4. Key Evidence Gaps and Considerations for Trial Design

### 4.1 Critical Limitations of Existing Evidence

1. **Single-center dominance:** The overwhelming majority of RCT evidence comes from one group (Paterna/Tuttolomondo, University of Palermo). This is a significant concern for generalizability. Nearly all meta-analyses are dominated by these trials.

2. **No large multicenter, adequately powered, double-blind trial:** The SALT-HF trial (N=167) is the most rigorous multicenter trial but was designed for a surrogate endpoint (3h diuresis) and studied ambulatory patients, not acutely hospitalized ADHF patients.

3. **High heterogeneity in meta-analyses:** I² values >75% for several outcomes (LOS, sodium change, urine sodium) suggest important differences across trials that pooled estimates may obscure.

4. **No trial powered for hard clinical endpoints as primary outcome:** Mortality and readmission data come from trials not designed with these as primary endpoints. The mortality benefit (RR ~0.55 in meta-analyses) is implausibly large for a diuretic adjunct and may reflect bias.

5. **HSS concentration varies widely across trials:** From 1.4% to 10% NaCl, in volumes from 50 mL to 150 mL. There is no dose-finding data comparing different concentrations head-to-head (except within the Palermo protocol where concentration is adjusted to serum Na).

6. **No trial of single vs. multiple doses:** No trial has compared a single bolus of HSS vs. repeated dosing. The Paterna protocol uses twice-daily dosing for the duration of hospitalization, while SALT-HF used a single dose. This is directly relevant to your planned trial.

7. **Modern HF background therapy:** Most Paterna trials predate SGLT2 inhibitors, sacubitril/valsartan, and current guideline-directed medical therapy. The DAPA-TONIC trial will partially address this but is limited to diabetic patients.

8. **Mechanism remains unclear:** Whether the benefit is from osmotic fluid shift, chloride repletion, increased sodium delivery to the loop of Henle, or neurohormonal modulation is not established. The SALT-HF chloride post-hoc analysis (PMID: 39933832) is the first to formally evaluate the chloride hypothesis.

9. **Limited data in HFpEF:** Most trials enrolled HFrEF (EF <35–40%). SALT-HF included 50% HFpEF but was not powered for subgroup analysis.

10. **No North American confirmatory trial completed and published:** The REaCH trial (N=50, Wisconsin) completed but results were never published in a peer-reviewed journal. The Barnes-Jewish trial was terminated. Griffin et al.'s retrospective U.S. experience is the only published U.S. data.

### 4.2 Specific Gaps Relevant to a Single/Double Dose Trial

- **Dose-response data:** Tuttolomondo 2015 (PMID: 26521190) is the only trial examining the dose-response curve. It showed HSS shifted the curve upward, but did not compare single vs. repeated HSS doses.
- **Optimal timing:** No data on whether a single early HSS dose at presentation captures most of the benefit vs. repeated dosing.
- **Spot urine sodium as a guide:** The DECONGEST trial used urine sodium to guide diuretic escalation (including HSS). This may inform whether HSS should be protocolized or given based on diuretic response.
- **Safety of HSS in hypernatremia:** Most trials excluded patients with Na >145. The safety threshold for hypertonic saline in eunatremic or mildly hypernatremic patients is not well established.
- **Interaction with SGLT2 inhibitors:** Unknown. DAPA-TONIC will provide the first data.

### 4.3 Key Design Considerations from the Literature

- **Concentration:** The Palermo protocol (1.4–4.6% adjusted to serum Na) is the most studied. SALT-HF used 2.6–3.4%. The Brazilian group used 7.5% in smaller volume.
- **Comparator:** Normal saline (0.9%) or glucose/dextrose infusion have been used. The choice of comparator matters for blinding and for the chloride hypothesis.
- **Population:** Ambulatory worsening HF (SALT-HF) vs. hospitalized ADHF (Paterna) — the populations are distinct and may respond differently.
- **Primary endpoint:** 3-hour or 24-hour diuresis/natriuresis (SALT-HF approach) is practical for an acute intervention. Weight change, congestion scores, and NTproBNP are reasonable secondary endpoints. Hard clinical endpoints require much larger samples.

## 5. Narrative Reviews and Mechanistic Papers

Several narrative reviews provide context on the mechanistic rationale:

- **Paterna & Di Pasquale (2007)**, Congest Heart Fail (2-part series, PMIDs: 17392613, 17541312): Reviews the sodium management rationale and neurohormonal effects.
- **Costanzo (2020)**, JACC Heart Fail ([DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jchf.2019.11.010)): Editorial accompanying the Griffin U.S. experience, discussing the genesis of fluid exodus with HSS.
- **Agrawal et al. (2024)**, Indian Heart J (PMID: 39579977): Small case series (N=10) of HSS in refractory ADHF from India, reporting faster decongestion without worsening renal function.
- **Redondo-Bermejo et al. (2024)**, Nefrologia (PMID: 38964947): Explores the "sodium-centric" vs. "chloride-centric" mechanistic debate — arguing chloride repletion may be the key mechanism rather than sodium per se.
- **Mullens et al. (2019)** and others on diuretic resistance: Place HSS in the broader context of strategies for diuretic-resistant AHF (sequential nephron blockade, acetazolamide, SGLT2i).
