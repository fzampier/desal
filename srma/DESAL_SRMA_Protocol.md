## 2. INTRODUCTION

### 2a. Rationale

Acute decompensated heart failure (ADHF) is a leading cause of hospitalization worldwide and is associated with substantial morbidity and mortality. Intravenous loop diuretics remain the cornerstone of decongestion therapy, yet a significant proportion of patients exhibit incomplete decongestion, diuretic resistance, or worsening renal function during treatment.

The co-administration of small-volume hypertonic saline solution (HSS) with loop diuretics has been proposed as a strategy to enhance diuretic efficacy. The physiological rationale rests on several mechanisms: HSS may increase intravascular volume transiently, improving renal perfusion and glomerular filtration rate; promote fluid redistribution from the interstitial to the intravascular compartment via osmotic gradients; enhance tubular delivery of diuretic to the loop of Henle; and counteract diuretic-induced hyponatremia.

Several randomized controlled trials, predominantly from a single research group based in Palermo, Italy (Paterna, Tuttolomondo, and colleagues), have reported striking benefits of HSS co-administration, including reduced mortality, shorter hospital stays, and improved urine output. However, the generalizability and robustness of these findings have been questioned given the concentration of evidence from a single center and research group.

A rigorous systematic review and meta-analysis, incorporating trial sequential analysis to assess the sufficiency of the accumulated evidence, is needed to determine the current state of evidence, identify gaps, and inform the design of future adequately powered multicenter trials.

### 2b. Objectives

The primary objective is to determine the effect of intravenous hypertonic saline co-administered with loop diuretics compared with loop diuretics alone (with or without isotonic saline or placebo) on all-cause mortality in adults hospitalized with ADHF.

Secondary objectives include assessment of effects on length of hospital stay, heart failure readmission, body weight change, urine output, renal function, serum sodium, natriuretic peptides, hypernatremia events, and acute kidney injury.

An additional objective is to perform trial sequential analysis to evaluate whether the accumulated evidence is sufficient to draw firm conclusions or whether further trials are needed, thereby informing the rationale for the planned DESAL trial.

## 3. METHODS

### 3a. Eligibility Criteria

#### Population

Adults (aged ≥18 years) hospitalized with a primary diagnosis of acute decompensated heart failure, regardless of left ventricular ejection fraction, etiology of heart failure, or baseline New York Heart Association (NYHA) functional class.

**Exclusions:**

- Ambulatory or outpatient heart failure management
- Pediatric populations (age <18 years)
- Heart failure in the immediate post-cardiac surgery setting

#### Intervention

Intravenous hypertonic saline solution at any concentration above 0.9% NaCl (e.g., 1.4%, 2.4%, 3%, 4.6%, 7.5%), co-administered with intravenous loop diuretics (furosemide, bumetanide, torsemide, or ethacrynic acid), at any dose, frequency, or duration.

#### Comparator

Intravenous loop diuretics alone, or co-administered with isotonic saline (0.9% NaCl), or co-administered with placebo. Any regimen that does not include hypertonic saline qualifies as a comparator.

#### Outcomes

**Primary outcome:**

- All-cause mortality (in-hospital or at longest available follow-up)

**Secondary outcomes:**

- Length of hospital stay (days)
- Heart failure readmission (at longest available follow-up)
- Change in body weight from baseline (kg)
- 24-hour or 48-hour urine output (mL)
- Change in serum creatinine from baseline (mg/dL or µmol/L)
- Change in serum sodium from baseline (mEq/L)
- Change in BNP or NT-proBNP from baseline (pg/mL)
- Hypernatremia events (serum sodium >145 mEq/L)
- Acute kidney injury (as defined by study authors)

#### Study types

Randomized controlled trials (RCTs), including individually randomized, cluster-randomized, and quasi-randomized trials. Crossover RCTs will be included using first-period data only; if only combined crossover data are available, the study will be included in a sensitivity analysis (see Section 4d). Dose-finding and pharmacokinetic studies will be included if they incorporate a comparator group and report clinical outcomes. Observational studies, case reports, and case series will be excluded. There will be no restriction by language, publication date, or publication status.

#### Setting

The primary analysis includes studies of **hospitalized** adults with acute decompensated heart failure. Studies enrolling exclusively ambulatory or day-hospital patients with worsening heart failure (not admitted to hospital) do not meet the population criterion and will be excluded from the primary analysis. However, if such trials otherwise meet all PICO criteria and report relevant outcomes, they will be included in a pre-specified sensitivity analysis broadening the population to include ambulatory worsening heart failure requiring intravenous diuretics (see Section 4d).

#### Overlapping cohorts

Multiple publications from the same research group (particularly the Paterna/Tuttolomondo group, University of Palermo) may include overlapping patient cohorts. The following decision rules will be applied:

1. For each research group and center, all publications will be mapped to determine which represent independent trial cohorts versus overlapping or extended follow-up of the same cohort.
2. When multiple publications clearly share the same cohort (e.g., short-term and long-term follow-up reports), only the publication with (a) the largest sample size, (b) the longest follow-up, and (c) the most complete outcome reporting will be included. The others will be listed as companion publications.
3. When overlap is suspected but cannot be confirmed from the publications (e.g., similar enrollment periods, overlapping sample sizes, same center), the authors will be contacted for clarification. If clarification cannot be obtained, the publication with the larger sample and more complete reporting will be included in the primary analysis, and a sensitivity analysis will be conducted including versus excluding the potentially overlapping publication.
4. Publications reporting different outcomes from the same trial (e.g., one reporting mortality, another reporting echocardiographic endpoints) will be treated as a single study, with data extracted from all companion publications for the relevant outcomes.

#### Preferred outcome timepoints

When a study reports the same outcome at multiple timepoints, the following hierarchy will be applied:

- **Mortality and readmission:** Longest available follow-up (for primary analysis). In-hospital mortality will be extracted separately as a secondary outcome.
- **Length of stay:** Index hospitalization (single timepoint by definition).
- **Physiological outcomes** (weight, urine output, creatinine, sodium, BNP): Timepoint closest to 48–72 hours (reflecting acute treatment response). If multiple timepoints are reported, all will be extracted but the preferred timepoint will be used for meta-analysis. Alternative timepoints will be explored in sensitivity analyses if data permit.

### 3d. Study Records

#### Data management

Search results will be exported to a reference management software (e.g., EndNote or Zotero) and de-duplicated. Screening will be conducted using a systematic review management platform (e.g., Covidence or Rayyan).

#### Selection process

Study selection will proceed in two stages:

1. **Title and abstract screening:** Two reviewers will independently screen all titles and abstracts against the eligibility criteria, including study design (RCTs, quasi-randomized trials, and dose-finding studies with comparator groups as specified in Section 3a). Because no study design filter is applied at the search level, ineligible study designs will be excluded at this stage. Studies that are potentially eligible or where eligibility is uncertain will be advanced to full-text review.

2. **Full-text review:** Two reviewers will independently assess the full text of all potentially eligible studies against the pre-specified eligibility criteria. Reasons for exclusion will be documented.

Disagreements at either stage will be resolved by discussion between the two reviewers or, if consensus cannot be reached, by adjudication from a third reviewer.

The technical specification of the dual-LLM screening and extraction pipeline used to operationalize this selection process is detailed in Appendix C.

A PRISMA 2020 flow diagram will be generated to document the study selection process, including the number of records identified, screened, assessed for eligibility, included, and excluded (with reasons).

#### Data extraction

Two reviewers will independently extract data using a standardized, pilot-tested data extraction form. Discrepancies will be resolved by discussion or a third reviewer.

**Data items:**

*Study characteristics:*

- First author, publication year, country
- Study design (parallel, crossover, factorial)
- Single-center vs. multicenter
- Sample size (total and per arm)
- Funding source
- Registration number (if available)

*Patient characteristics (per arm, as available):*

- Age (mean or median)
- Sex distribution (% female)
- Left ventricular ejection fraction
- Baseline serum sodium
- Baseline serum creatinine
- Baseline BNP or NT-proBNP
- NYHA functional class distribution
- Heart failure etiology
- Baseline diuretic dose

*Intervention details:*

- Hypertonic saline concentration (%)
- Volume administered per dose (mL)
- Frequency of administration
- Total duration of treatment
- Co-administered loop diuretic (drug, dose, route, frequency)
- Other co-interventions (e.g., fluid restriction, oral potassium supplementation)

*Comparator details:*

- Fluid co-administered (isotonic saline, no fluid, placebo)
- Loop diuretic (drug, dose, route, frequency)
- Other co-interventions

*Outcome data:*

- All outcomes listed in Section 3a, with sample sizes, means (SD), medians (IQR), event counts, and denominators, as reported
- Time points of outcome assessment
- Definitions used for composite or subjective outcomes

### 3e. Risk of Bias Assessment

Risk of bias will be assessed independently by two reviewers using the Cochrane Risk of Bias 2.0 (RoB 2) tool for randomized trials. RoB 2 evaluates five domains:

1. Bias arising from the randomization process
2. Bias due to deviations from intended interventions
3. Bias due to missing outcome data
4. Bias in measurement of the outcome
5. Bias in selection of the reported result

Each domain will be judged as "low risk of bias," "some concerns," or "high risk of bias." An overall risk of bias judgment will be assigned for each study-outcome combination. Disagreements will be resolved by discussion or a third reviewer.

### 3f. Certainty of Evidence

The certainty (quality) of evidence for each outcome will be assessed using the Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development and Evaluation (GRADE) approach. For each outcome, the body of evidence from RCTs will start at high certainty and may be downgraded based on:

- Risk of bias
- Inconsistency
- Indirectness
- Imprecision
- Publication bias

Results will be presented in a Summary of Findings table.

## 4. DATA SYNTHESIS AND STATISTICAL ANALYSIS

### 4a. Meta-Analysis

All statistical analyses will be conducted in R (version ≥4.0) using the `meta` and `metafor` packages.

**Effect measures:**

- Dichotomous outcomes (mortality, readmission, hypernatremia, AKI): Risk Ratios (RR) with 95% confidence intervals
- Continuous outcomes (LOS, weight change, urine output, creatinine change, sodium change, BNP change): Mean Differences (MD) with 95% confidence intervals

Where studies report medians and interquartile ranges, means and standard deviations will be estimated using validated methods (e.g., Wan et al., BMC Med Res Methodol, 2014; Luo et al., Stat Methods Med Res, 2018).

**Model:**

A random-effects model using the restricted maximum likelihood (REML) estimator for between-study variance (tau-squared) will be used for all analyses. This accounts for expected clinical and methodological heterogeneity across studies.

**Heterogeneity assessment:**

- Cochran's Q test (p <0.10 considered significant)
- I-squared statistic: 0-25% low, 26-50% moderate, 51-75% substantial, >75% considerable heterogeneity
- Prediction intervals will be reported where feasible

**Zero-event handling:**

For binary outcomes (mortality, readmission, hypernatremia, AKI), studies with zero events in both arms will be excluded from the primary analysis, as the risk ratio is undefined. Studies with zero events in one arm only will be included using a 0.5 continuity correction (default in the `meta` package). A sensitivity analysis using the treatment-arm continuity correction (TACC, which is proportional to the reciprocal of the opposite arm's sample size) will be conducted to assess robustness.

**Small-study effects:**

If 10 or more studies are available for an outcome, funnel plot asymmetry will be assessed visually and formally using Egger's regression test.

### 4b. Trial Sequential Analysis

Trial sequential analysis (TSA) will be performed for the primary outcome (all-cause mortality) and key secondary outcomes (length of hospital stay, heart failure readmission).

**TSA parameters:**

- Alpha spending function: O'Brien-Fleming boundaries
- Type I error (alpha): 5% (two-sided)
- Type II error (beta): 20% (power = 80%)
- Relative risk reduction (RRR): Derived from the pooled estimate of the conventional random-effects meta-analysis
- Control event rate: Derived from the pooled control arm event rate across included studies
- Heterogeneity adjustment: Model-variance-based (D-squared from the meta-analysis)

TSA will be implemented using custom R code and all code will be made publicly available.

The required information size (RIS) — the meta-analytic equivalent of a sample size calculation — will be computed. If the cumulative Z-curve crosses the TSA monitoring boundary, the result will be considered conclusive. If it does not, the evidence will be considered inconclusive and the deficit between the current information size and the RIS will be reported to inform future trial planning (specifically DESAL).

### 4c. Subgroup Analyses

Pre-specified subgroup analyses will be conducted using random-effects meta-regression or stratified meta-analysis if a sufficient number of studies (at least 2 per subgroup) is available:

1. **HSS concentration:** 3% or less vs. greater than 3%
2. **Dosing frequency:** Single dose vs. repeated doses
3. **Baseline serum sodium:** 135 mEq/L or less (hyponatremic) vs. greater than 135 mEq/L — if reported
4. **Heart failure phenotype:** HFrEF (EF 40% or less) vs. HFpEF (EF greater than 40%) — if reported
5. **Risk of bias:** Low risk of bias vs. high risk or some concerns (per RoB 2 overall judgment)
6. **Research group:** Paterna/Tuttolomondo group (Palermo, Italy) vs. independent research groups
7. **Diuretic-resistant vs. unselected ADHF populations:** Based on whether the trial's inclusion criteria required demonstrated diuretic resistance or enrolled any hospitalized ADHF patient regardless of diuretic response

Subgroup analysis #6 is a key sensitivity analysis given the concentration of published evidence from this single research group. The interaction test (test for subgroup differences) will be reported for each subgroup analysis.

### 4d. Sensitivity Analyses

The following pre-specified sensitivity analyses will be conducted for all outcomes:

1. **Excluding Paterna/Tuttolomondo group studies:** To assess whether the overall results are driven by this single center
2. **Excluding high risk of bias studies:** Restricted to studies with overall low risk of bias per RoB 2
3. **Fixed-effect model:** Using the inverse-variance fixed-effect model as an alternative to random-effects
4. **Leave-one-out analysis:** Sequentially excluding each study to assess the influence of individual studies on the pooled estimate
5. **Broadened population:** Including ambulatory worsening heart failure trials (e.g., SALT-HF) that otherwise meet PICO criteria but enroll non-hospitalized patients requiring IV diuretics, to assess whether the hospitalization requirement materially changes the pooled estimate
6. **Crossover trials:** Excluding crossover trials or, if first-period data are unavailable, excluding studies where only combined crossover data could be used
7. **Treatment-arm continuity correction:** For binary outcomes, replacing the default 0.5 continuity correction with the treatment-arm continuity correction (TACC) to assess sensitivity of results to zero-event handling
8. **Alternative timepoints:** For physiological outcomes reported at multiple timepoints, using the alternative timepoint (e.g., 24h instead of 48-72h) to assess whether timing of outcome assessment affects the pooled estimate

### Context

This document describes the complete LLM-assisted pipeline for conducting a systematic review and meta-analysis (SR/MA) of hypertonic saline for acute decompensated heart failure (ADHF). The pipeline uses two LLMs — Claude (via Claude Code / Cowork) and GPT-5.4 (via Codex) — as independent reviewers, with the goal of reducing human burden while maintaining the rigor expected of a Cochrane-quality SR/MA.

The dual-LLM approach serves two purposes: (1) a practical tool for this specific SR/MA, and (2) a publishable methodological innovation demonstrating how LLM-assisted systematic reviews can be conducted with appropriate safeguards. The methodology itself may warrant a standalone methods paper.

The clinical protocol for this SR/MA, including PICO criteria, search strategy, and statistical analysis plan, is documented in the preceding sections of this protocol.

#### 1.2 Export and De-duplication

Records are exported in RIS or CSV format with the following minimum fields: title, abstract, authors, year, journal, DOI, PMID.

De-duplication is automated:

1. **Exact DOI match** — Remove duplicates sharing the same DOI across databases.
2. **Exact PMID match** — Remove duplicates sharing the same PMID.
3. **Fuzzy title matching** — For records lacking DOI/PMID, flag potential duplicates using normalized title similarity (Levenshtein distance ≤ 3 after lowercasing and removing punctuation). These are reviewed manually.

Output: a single deduplicated CSV file with one row per unique citation, ready for screening.

### 2. Screening Phase

**Status: Built.** Pre-specified before data exposure.

The screening phase replaces the traditional dual-human reviewer approach with dual-LLM screening, followed by targeted human review. Both models receive identical inputs and prompts; disagreements are escalated to human adjudication.

#### 2.1 Title/Abstract Screening

Both Claude and GPT-5.4 independently screen each citation. They receive the same system prompt containing the PICO criteria:

**PICO Criteria:**

- **P:** Adults ≥18 years hospitalized with acute decompensated heart failure
- **I:** Intravenous hypertonic saline (>0.9% NaCl) co-administered with loop diuretics
- **C:** Loop diuretics alone ± isotonic saline or placebo (no hypertonic saline)
- **O:** All-cause mortality, length of stay, readmission, renal function, diuretic response, safety events
- **Study type:** Randomized controlled trials only

Each model outputs structured JSON per citation:

```json
{
  "pmid": "12345678",
  "decision": "include | exclude | uncertain",
  "confidence": 0.0-1.0,
  "rationale": "Brief explanation of decision",
  "pico_match": {
    "population": true,
    "intervention": true,
    "comparator": true,
    "outcome": true,
    "study_type": true
  }
}
```

The `pico_match` object provides transparency into which criteria drove the decision. A citation that fails on `study_type` (not an RCT) vs. `intervention` (no hypertonic saline) generates different rationales, which matters for auditing systematic errors.

#### 2.2 Resolution Logic

| Model A (Claude) | Model B (GPT-5.4) | Resolution |
|---|---|---|
| INCLUDE | INCLUDE | Auto-include |
| EXCLUDE | EXCLUDE | Auto-exclude (subject to 10% audit) |
| INCLUDE | EXCLUDE | → Human review |
| EXCLUDE | INCLUDE | → Human review |
| UNCERTAIN | Any | → Human review |
| Any | UNCERTAIN | → Human review |

This is conservative by design. The only citations that bypass human eyes entirely are those where both models agree to include (low risk — erring on the side of inclusion) or both agree to exclude (audited at 10%, see Section 2.3).

#### 2.3 Human Audit of Auto-Excludes

A 10% random sample of auto-excluded citations (both models said EXCLUDE) is reviewed by a human reviewer (Fernando). This serves three purposes:

1. **Calculate sensitivity/specificity** of the dual-LLM screening against the human gold standard on this subset.
2. **Detect systematic blind spots** where both models agree incorrectly — e.g., consistently excluding non-English studies, or missing studies that use uncommon terminology for hypertonic saline.
3. **Calibrate confidence** — if the 10% audit reveals any missed eligible studies, expand to 20% or full human review of auto-excludes.

Sampling is simple random (not stratified) since all citations come from the same auto-exclude pool. The audit sample size depends on the total number of auto-excludes; with the expected yield of ~200-500 citations total, a 10% audit means reviewing ~15-40 citations manually, which is feasible.

#### 2.4 Full-Text Screening

Citations classified as INCLUDE or UNCERTAIN after title/abstract screening advance to full-text review. The same dual-LLM approach is applied:

1. Full-text PDFs are converted to text using `pdftotext`.
2. Both models receive the extracted text + the same PICO prompt (expanded to include additional exclusion criteria).
3. Same resolution logic from Section 2.2 applies.

**Additional exclusion criteria checked at full-text stage:**

- Not hospitalized acute heart failure (e.g., chronic outpatient HF)
- Not a randomized controlled trial (e.g., quasi-randomized, observational)
- No hypertonic saline intervention (>0.9% NaCl)
- Duplicate publication of same trial data
- Conference abstract only without sufficient data for extraction
- Pediatric population

#### 2.5 Reporting Metrics

The following metrics are reported for the screening phase:

**Inter-model agreement:**

- Cohen's kappa for the binary include/exclude decision (treating UNCERTAIN as a separate category, or collapsing UNCERTAIN into INCLUDE for a more conservative estimate)
- Proportion of citations where both models agreed vs. disagreed
- Distribution of disagreement types (which model was more liberal/conservative)

**Accuracy vs. human audit:**

- Sensitivity: proportion of truly eligible studies correctly auto-included by both models
- Specificity: proportion of truly ineligible studies correctly auto-excluded by both models
- False negative rate from the 10% audit (missed eligible studies)

**PRISMA flow diagram:**

The standard PRISMA 2020 flow diagram is annotated with LLM-specific information:

- Number of citations screened by each model
- Number auto-resolved (both agree) vs. escalated to human
- Number from the 10% audit that changed classification
- Final included/excluded counts with reasons

### 3. Extraction Phase

The extraction phase combines the existing `clinical-data-extractor` skill (a multi-layer verification system already built and tested) with new orchestration components that coordinate dual-model extraction and automated disagreement resolution.

#### 3.1 Existing Skill: clinical-data-extractor

**Location:** `~/Desktop/DESAL/clinical-data-extractor/`
**Status:** Built and tested on 6S, 3CPO, and ANIST trials.

The skill implements four verification layers, each targeting different failure modes of LLM-generated numerical claims:

**Layer 1: Anchor-Based Extraction (`anchor_extract.py`)**

Constrains extraction upfront rather than filtering afterward. Numbers are only extracted when they appear within a character window (250-600 chars) of semantically relevant anchor terms. For example, mortality anchors include "mortality", "death(s)", "died", "survival", "fatal" — so the number "62" from "SAPS II 51 (39-62)" is never extracted when looking for mortality data.

This is the inverse of traditional extraction: instead of extracting all numbers and then verifying, it only pulls numbers that appear in the right context.

**Layer 2: Text Verification (`verify_numbers.py`)**

Checks whether claimed numbers actually exist in the source text. Catches completely invented numbers, transposed digits, and wrong units. Output categories:

- ✓ VERIFIED: Number found in source text
- ✗ UNVERIFIED: Number not found (likely fabrication)
- ⚠️ CITATION_ONLY: Found only in reference section (suspicious)

**Layer 3: Table Verification (`verify_with_tables.py`)**

The most important layer for human-aided review. Extracts tables from PDFs (using `extract_tables.py` with camelot-py and pdfplumber as fallback), then shows WHERE each number appears in context. This enables rapid human verification (~5 seconds per claim):

```
✓ '62' found at:
    → Page 3: "SAPS II 51 (39-62)"     ← Obviously not mortality!
```

This catches semantic mismatches where a number exists in the paper but in the wrong context — the hardest type of error for automated systems to detect.

**Layer 4: Benford's Law Check (`benford_check.py`)**

For large extraction datasets (50+ numbers), checks whether the distribution of leading digits follows Benford's Law. Natural data has ~30% leading 1s and ~5% leading 9s; fabricated "random" numbers cluster in middle digits (4-7). Catches systematic fabrication but not individual errors.

**Measured Detection Rates:**

| Method | Auto-Caught | Flagged for Review | With Human Review |
|---|---|---|---|
| Text verification only (v1) | 33% | 0% | 33% |
| Text + context display (v2) | 50% | 17% | 100% |
| Tables + context display | 50% | 17% | 100% |

Key finding: context display enables 100% detection with human review, at ~5 seconds per flagged claim.

#### 3.3 Cross-Model Orchestration

**Status: Built.** Pre-specified before data exposure.

This is the core pipeline that coordinates dual-model extraction with multi-layer verification. For each included study:

```
Step 1: PDF → text extraction (pdftotext) + table extraction (extract_tables.py)
        ↓
Step 2: Model A (Claude) extracts into Pydantic schema
        ↓
Step 3: Run existing skill verification (Layers 1-3) on Model A output
        ↓
Step 4: Model B (GPT-5.4 via Codex) extracts into same Pydantic schema
        ↓
Step 5: Run existing skill verification (Layers 1-3) on Model B output
        ↓
Step 6: Cell-by-cell comparison of Model A vs. Model B outputs
        ↓
Step 7: Disagreement classification (Section 3.4)
        ↓
Step 8: LLM auditor triages discrepancies (Section 3.5)
        ↓
Step 9: Human reviews remaining conflicts
```

Steps 2-3 and 4-5 can run in parallel since the two models are independent. Step 6 onward is sequential.

The orchestration script manages the full pipeline for a batch of studies, tracks status per study, and produces a summary report of extraction quality metrics.

#### 3.4 Disagreement Classifier

**Status: Built.** Pre-specified before data exposure.

After both models extract data for a study, each cell (field) is compared and classified into one of six disagreement levels:

| Level | Name | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | Perfect agreement | Same value, both verified by skill | Both say mortality = 15/108 |
| 1 | Trivial difference | Rounding, units, formatting | 3.2 vs. 3.20, or "mg" vs. "milligrams" |
| 2 | Minor difference | Close values, likely from different table/text locations | Mean age 62.3 vs. 63.1 |
| 3 | Moderate difference | Different values, one verified and one not, or both verified from different contexts | LOS 8.5 vs. 9.2, one from abstract, one from table |
| 4 | Major difference | Contradictory values, both models confident | Mortality 15% vs. 23% |
| 5 | Structural disagreement | One model found data the other said was not reported | Model A: readmission = 12/54; Model B: "not reported" |

**Resolution rules:**

| Level | Action |
|---|---|
| 0-1 | Auto-accept. Use the verified value (Level 0) or the more precise value (Level 1). |
| 2 | Accept if both verified by skill. Flag for human review if only one verified. |
| 3-5 | Route to LLM auditor (Section 3.5). If auditor confidence < 0.8, route to human review with full context from both models + verification layers. |

The classifier is deterministic — it compares values numerically (with configurable tolerance for Level 1-2 boundaries) and checks verification status from the skill output.

#### 3.5 LLM Auditor Layer

**Status: Built.** Pre-specified before data exposure.

A third LLM call (using either Claude or GPT-5.4, alternating to avoid self-bias) that serves as an automated triage step before human review. The auditor receives:

- The original source text and extracted tables
- Both models' extractions for the disputed field(s)
- The verification results from the clinical-data-extractor skill (Layers 1-3)
- The disagreement classification level

The auditor outputs:

```json
{
  "field": "mortality_intervention",
  "model_a_value": 15,
  "model_b_value": 23,
  "recommended_value": 15,
  "recommendation_source": "Table 2, page 5",
  "confidence": 0.95,
  "rationale": "Model A value matches Table 2 exactly. Model B appears to have extracted from the combined mortality endpoint in the discussion.",
  "human_review_needed": false
}
```

The auditor partially replaces the human in the Layer 3 loop. It triages which discrepancies genuinely need expert judgment (ambiguous source data, genuinely conflicting reports in the paper) vs. which are resolvable by careful re-reading of the source.

Expected outcome: the auditor resolves ~60-70% of Level 2-3 disagreements automatically, reducing human review to Level 4-5 disagreements and low-confidence auditor outputs.

### 4. Analysis Phase

All analysis code will be written in R and made publicly available.

#### 4.1 Meta-Analysis

**R packages:** `meta`, `metafor`

**Model:** Random-effects with restricted maximum likelihood (REML) estimator for tau-squared.

**Effect measures:**

- Dichotomous outcomes (mortality, readmission, hypernatremia, AKI): Risk Ratios (RR) with 95% CI
- Continuous outcomes (LOS, weight change, urine output, creatinine change, sodium change, BNP change): Mean Differences (MD) with 95% CI

Where studies report medians and IQRs, means and SDs will be estimated using validated methods (Wan et al., 2014; Luo et al., 2018).

**Heterogeneity:** Cochran's Q test (p < 0.10), I² statistic, prediction intervals.

**Small-study effects:** Funnel plots + Egger's regression test if ≥10 studies for an outcome.

**Outputs:** Forest plots, funnel plots per outcome.

#### 4.2 Trial Sequential Analysis

**Status: Built.** Pre-specified before data exposure.
**Location:** `~/Desktop/DESAL/analysis/R/tsa.R`

TSA will be implemented in custom R code, not the Copenhagen TSA software. This is intentional — custom code allows full transparency, reproducibility, and integration with the meta-analysis pipeline.

**Parameters:**

- Alpha spending function: O'Brien-Fleming boundaries
- Type I error: 5% (two-sided)
- Type II error: 20% (power = 80%)
- Relative risk reduction: derived from the pooled random-effects estimate
- Control event rate: pooled from control arms of included studies
- Heterogeneity adjustment: D² from the meta-analysis

**Interpretation:**

- If cumulative Z-curve crosses the TSA monitoring boundary → evidence is conclusive
- If it does not → evidence is inconclusive; the gap between current information size and the required information size (RIS) directly informs DESAL sample size planning

#### 4.3 Subgroup and Sensitivity Analyses

**Pre-specified subgroup analyses (at least 2 studies per subgroup required):**

1. HSS concentration: ≤3% vs. >3%
2. Dosing frequency: single dose vs. repeated doses
3. Baseline serum sodium: ≤135 mEq/L vs. >135 mEq/L
4. Heart failure phenotype: HFrEF (EF ≤40%) vs. HFpEF (EF >40%)
5. Risk of bias: low vs. some concerns/high (per RoB 2 overall judgment)
6. **Research group: Paterna/Tuttolomondo (Palermo) vs. independent groups** — This is the key sensitivity analysis, given that the majority of published evidence comes from a single research group.
7. **Diuretic-resistant populations vs. unselected ADHF populations** — based on whether the trial's inclusion criteria required demonstrated diuretic resistance (e.g., minimum prior diuretic dose, documented inadequate response) or enrolled any hospitalized ADHF patient regardless of diuretic response

Interaction tests will be reported for each subgroup analysis.

**Pre-specified sensitivity analyses:**

1. Excluding all Paterna/Tuttolomondo group studies
2. Excluding high risk of bias studies
3. Fixed-effect model (inverse-variance) as alternative to random-effects
4. Leave-one-out analysis
5. Broadened population (including ambulatory worsening HF trials)
6. Excluding crossover trials (or studies with combined crossover data only)
7. Treatment-arm continuity correction (TACC) for binary outcomes
8. Alternative outcome timepoints for physiological outcomes

#### 4.4 GRADE Assessment

Certainty of evidence assessed per outcome using the GRADE framework. RCT evidence starts at high certainty and is downgraded across five domains:

1. **Risk of bias** — informed by RoB 2.0 assessments
2. **Inconsistency** — informed by I², prediction intervals, subgroup analyses
3. **Indirectness** — population, intervention, comparator, outcome directness
4. **Imprecision** — width of confidence intervals, whether the interval crosses clinically meaningful thresholds
5. **Publication bias** — funnel plot asymmetry, Egger's test, whether most evidence comes from a single group

Results presented in a Summary of Findings table.

#### 6.2 Potential Standalone Methods Paper

The LLM-assisted SR/MA methodology is independently publishable. A methods paper would cover:

- Rationale for dual-LLM over single-LLM with human
- The screening protocol with resolution logic
- The extraction pipeline with multi-layer verification
- The disagreement classification system
- Empirical results: agreement rates, error types caught, time savings
- Comparison with existing tools (e.g., ASReview, Rayyan AI features)

#### 6.3 Limitations to Acknowledge

- **Correlated errors between LLMs:** Both models share training data and may have similar blind spots. The 10% audit partially addresses this but cannot fully eliminate the risk.
- **Model version dependency:** Results are specific to Claude and GPT-5.4 as of the study date. Different model versions may produce different results. Exact model versions and API parameters must be reported.
- **Reproducibility:** LLM outputs are stochastic. Temperature settings, system prompts, and random seeds (where available) must be documented. Running the same pipeline twice may yield slightly different screening decisions.
- **Cost and access:** Requires API access to two commercial LLMs, which limits accessibility compared to free tools.
- **Validation scope:** The 10% audit of auto-excludes provides a point estimate of screening accuracy but has limited statistical power to detect rare systematic errors.
- **Not a replacement for domain expertise:** The LLM auditor and disagreement classifier reduce but do not eliminate the need for human judgment, particularly for ambiguous source data and clinical context.
